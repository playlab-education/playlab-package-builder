Claude

Claude Cowork is for everyone — it lets you delegate tasks to Claude using connected tools like Gmail, Calendar, and Drive. Claude Code is the developer-facing terminal tool that powers Cowork under the hood. Same engine, different interfaces. Most of you will use Cowork; the Power Users tab includes Claude Code examples to show what’s possible at the edges.

Skills are slash commands you can type in Cowork tasks, or Claude will use them automatically based on context. You can run multiple tasks simultaneously. Learn more.

To get started, download the desktop app at claude.com/download and toggle from “Chat” to “Cowork” at the top of the app.

Claude Cowork Skills for Playlab

These are the Cowork skills built specifically for the Playlab team. Each skill is a slash command you can type in a Cowork task — or Claude will use them automatically based on context. Skills are organized by how your day flows.

Planning & Big Picture

Start your day, end your week, stay on top of what matters

/daily-briefing Morning Executive Briefing

Generate a morning executive briefing — a concise, prioritized snapshot of your day. Pulls calendar, email, and messaging data to surface what matters before you start.

Try it: /daily-briefing Give me my morning briefing Briefing for tomorrow What does my day look like Monday?
/weekly-recap End-of-Week Summary

Generate a concise end-of-week summary: meetings held, key emails sent, action items still open, and threads that have gone cold. Helps with Friday wrap-up or Monday planning.

Try it: /weekly-recap Give me my weekly recap Recap last week What happened this week?
🔍

Meeting Preparation

Show up informed and ready for every conversation

/meeting-prep-briefing Pre-Meeting Briefing

Generate a pre-meeting briefing for an upcoming call or meeting. Pulls context from calendar and email history to give you a clear picture before you walk in — attendee info, relationship history, open items, and talking points.

Try it: /meeting-prep-briefing Prep my next meeting Prep Sarah Chen from Acme Meeting prep for tomorrow
/meeting-scheduler Calendar Availability & Scheduling

Check your calendar availability and draft a scheduling email — with context pulled from prior conversations so the invite isn’t cold. Handles new vs. existing contacts differently.

Try it: /meeting-scheduler Schedule 30 min with Sarah next week Find time for a call with the Acme team Book a meeting with James — pilot kickoff

After Meetings

Nothing falls through the cracks after a conversation

/meeting-follow-up-email Follow-Up Email Drafter

Draft follow-up emails after meetings. Pulls recent calendar events, enriches with context, then drafts a follow-up in your voice. Shows status and recommendations (needed / simple touch / already sent / skip).

Try it: /meeting-follow-up-email Follow up on my last meeting Draft follow-ups for this week Follow up with Sarah about next steps
/meeting-action-items Action Item Extractor

Pull today’s meetings, extract all action items assigned to you, and present a clean summary. Gathers from meeting notes, sent emails, and calendar descriptions. Best run at end of day.

Try it: /meeting-action-items What are my action items from today? Pull action items from today’s meetings What did I commit to today?
/granola-hubspot-sync Meeting Notes to CRM

Sync your Granola meeting notes to HubSpot as properly formatted meeting engagements with correct date/time, attendee associations, and company links. Prevents duplicates.

Try it: /granola-hubspot-sync Sync today’s meetings to HubSpot Sync Granola notes from yesterday Sync this week’s meetings
/crm-update CRM Note Generator

After a meeting or email exchange, generate a structured CRM-ready note summarizing what happened, decisions made, and next steps. Works with any CRM — outputs formatted text or sends to a CRM forwarding address.

Try it: /crm-update CRM update for my call with Sarah today CRM update for my thread with Acme Log my last meeting to HubSpot

Context & Instructions

Give Cowork persistent context so it works the way you want every time

Global Instructions
In the Claude Desktop app, go to Settings > Cowork and edit “Global Instructions.” This is your always-on context: your role, preferred tone, output formats, and tools you use. Applies to every Cowork session automatically.
Project Instructions
Each Cowork Project has its own instructions field. Use this for workstream-specific context (e.g., “This project handles partnership proposals. Always use Playlab brand voice and reference our pricing tiers.”).
Context Files
Create markdown files in your working folder that Cowork reads automatically: about-me.md (your role and priorities), voice-and-style.md (tone and formatting), working-rules.md (behavior preferences). This is the Cowork equivalent of CLAUDE.md in Claude Code.

How Partners Come to Playlab
SourceWhat Happens
Inbound — WebsitePartner fills out the inquiry form (“Partner with Us” / “Contact Playlab”). Nate or Mera triages and assigns.
Inbound — ReferralAn existing partner, colleague, or network contact introduces someone. Often arrives via email or Slack.
Inbound — ConferenceSomeone connects with Playlab at an event — business card, booth conversation, session follow-up.
Outbound — ProspectingPlaylab identifies a potential partner and reaches out directly via email or LinkedIn.
Renewal / ExpansionAn existing partner is ready for a new engagement — either a renewal or expanded scope.
What Happens from Playlab’s Side
  1. 1Inquiry is triaged — Nate or Mera reviews the inquiry and determines if it’s a fit for Playlab
  2. 2Context is gathered — research the org (use /research [org name] in Claude Cowork), check HubSpot for prior history, review any materials they shared
  3. 3Playlab responds within 24 hours — use /email-draft in Claude Cowork (see Claude tab) to draft a warm, personalized response that acknowledges their context
  4. 4Discovery call is scheduled — include a Calendly link or propose specific times
  5. 5Internal assignment — Nate posts in #partnerships Slack with partner context, tags a suggested LP (or @Mera to assign). LP replies: “On it! Scheduling for [date].”
✏️
Manual: If a deal doesn’t exist yet, create one in HubSpot (or use /opp in Claude Cowork). If one already exists, change the deal owner to yourself and confirm the stage is “Discovery.” Add a note with how the partner came to us and any context from the inquiry.
🎯
The goal of this stage is simple: Respond quickly, make the partner feel heard, and get a discovery call on the calendar. Speed matters — inquiries go cold fast.
🚨 Escalate When
  • The inquiry is from a channel/intermediary org (they want to resell or bundle Playlab) — Nate handles these
  • The inquiry mentions an RFP, grant, or formal procurement process — different pipeline, Nate handles
  • You’re unsure if the inquiry is a fit for Playlab — check with Nate before responding
  • The partner is a high-profile org or >$40K estimated value — flag Nate early
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Waiting more than 24 hours to respond — momentum dies fast
  • Sending a generic response instead of referencing something specific about the partner
  • Not creating or updating the HubSpot deal — if it’s not in HubSpot, it doesn’t exist
  • Jumping straight to a demo or pricing without scheduling a proper discovery conversation
2
Discovery
The partner is exploring. This is where Playlab learns what they need.
▼
🔄
Renewals: For renewals, discovery is a check-in, not a first conversation. You already know them. Focus on: What went well this year? What do they want more or less of? Has their budget changed? Any new priorities for next year? Skip the ACE opener and demo — go straight to the relationship.
What Is This Stage?
The partner is curious about Playlab but hasn’t committed to anything. This conversation is about them — their priorities, pain points, AI journey, and decision-making process. No packages, no pricing. Ask questions first. Demo second.
Before the Call
  1. 1Research the org and person — use Claude Cowork /meeting-prep-briefing or check HubSpot, email, their website, and recent news
  2. 2Prep 2–3 example Playlab apps relevant to their context (from Playlab Gardens)
  3. 3Have an app idea ready to build live that relates to their role or priorities
The Call: 4 Steps
StepTimeWhat Happens
1. ACE Opener5 minAppreciate (“Thanks for your time”) → Check time (“We have 30 min, still work?”) → End goal (“I’d love to learn about your priorities and see if Playlab could help”)
2. Ask Questions20 minUse the questions below. The partner should be talking 60%+ of this time. One question at a time, then listen.
3. Demo Playlab10 minAsk permission: “Would it help to see Playlab in action?” Build an app live or walk through examples. They must see a build and a demo app.
4. Close5 minConfirm next steps. Schedule the follow-up. “What questions do you have? What’s resonating?”
The Questions to Ask (Before Demoing)

The Process
  1. 1Copy the SOW Builder link (the URL with your package configured)
  2. 2Go to #proposal-bot in Slack and share: the package URL, context (Granola notes link, bullet points, or an email), and what language the proposal should be in
  3. 3Review the generated proposal for accuracy — always proofread before sending
  4. 4Check the new HubSpot deal entry — the proposal bot creates or updates the deal automatically (see below). Verify it looks correct.
  5. 5If unsure about anything, post in #approval before sending
  6. 6Send the proposal to the partner within 24 hours — use /email-draft in Claude Cowork to draft the send email
  7. 7Set a calendar reminder to follow up in 3 business days if no response
⚙️
Automated — proposal bot does this: When you post in #proposal-bot, it generates a formatted Google Doc proposal AND creates/updates the HubSpot deal with the correct amount, pipeline stage, associated contacts, and a link to the proposal. You still need to review both outputs for accuracy.
✏️
Manual — you do this: After the proposal bot runs, verify the HubSpot deal: confirm stage is “Proposal - Drafted,” amount is correct, and the proposal link is attached. Fix any errors. The bot gets it right most of the time, but you own accuracy.
Proposal Framing
Most early-stage proposals should be framed as a “reaction document” — a conversation starter, not a ready-to-sign offer. Use language like: “Here are a few options to work from given your priorities and capacity.” Only frame as a formal proposal when the partner has clear scope, budget, and timeline.
🚨 Escalate When
  • Partner pushes back on pricing — don’t negotiate. Say: “Let me connect you with Nate to discuss options.”
  • Partner wants to significantly change scope after seeing the proposal
  • Partner goes silent for 2+ weeks — send a nudge first (/nudge in Claude Cowork drafts a re-engagement email), then flag Nate if still no response
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Not reviewing the proposal-bot output before sending — always proofread names, amounts, and package details
  • Letting the proposal sit in #proposal-bot without sending it — 24-hour rule
  • Not setting a follow-up reminder — proposals go cold fast
  • Negotiating pricing yourself instead of bringing Nate in

Contract & Close
The partner has approved. Now it’s contracts, logistics, and keeping momentum.
▼
What Is This Stage?
The partner has approved the proposal. Nate handles contract generation and negotiation. The rest of Playlab stays in touch with the partner, answers questions about the engagement (not the contract), and keeps momentum going.
The Process
  1. 1Confirm with Nate in Slack that the partner is ready for contract — share any nuances from your conversations
  2. 2Continue planning the engagement in parallel — discuss dates, logistics, and content with the partner
  3. 3Respond to partner questions about what the engagement will look like (dates, format, participants)
  4. 4If contract signing stalls, check with Nate on messaging before nudging the partner
⚙️
Nate manages HubSpot stages here: Contract Sent → Contract Signed → Invoice Sent → Payment Received. You do NOT need to move these stages — Nate handles them as contract milestones are hit.
✏️
Manual — you still do this: Keep adding notes to the HubSpot deal after every partner interaction. Even though Nate owns the contract stages, your engagement notes are critical for the full picture.
🚨 Escalate When
  • This entire stage is Nate-led — flag anything where the partner mentions concerns, cold feet, or competing options
  • Partner asks contract or legal questions — always route to Nate
  • Partner requests scope changes during contract negotiation — don’t agree to changes, bring Nate in
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Going dark during the contract phase — keep the relationship warm with planning conversations
  • Agreeing to scope changes or verbal commitments without checking with Nate
  • Assuming the contract is “Nate’s problem” and disengaging — you still own the relationship

  • Not updating HubSpot during delivery — renewal conversations rely on this history

Discount Policy

Strategic Principle: Protect Software, Flex on Services
  • First lever: Reduce services pricing (facilitation, coaching, tool builds)
  • Last lever: Reduce software/license pricing
  • Never: Discount software to close a services-only deal
Software tier pricing is already volume-discounted by design. The tiers ARE the discount — don’t layer additional reductions.
Core Principles:
  • Always show Standard Rate alongside the partner price
  • Name every discount — use the category names below
  • “Included” not “$0” — bundled services preserve perceived value
  • Services and software discount separately
  • Hard floor: 70% of Standard Rate. Nothing below this.
  • Discounts don’t stack. Use whichever is most favorable to the partner
  • Proposal pricing valid for 60 days
  • All discounts should be run through Nate before committing to the partner

When NOT to Discount:
  • Partner is grant-funded (the grant IS the subsidy)
  • Partner is asking for software discount on a services-heavy deal
  • Multiple discount categories would need to stack (pick one)
  • Partner needs below-floor pricing AND high-touch support
  • Partner wants perpetual pilot pricing beyond the first contract
In all cases: reduce scope before reducing rate.
